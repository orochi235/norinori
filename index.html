Norinori Solving Lemmas

A structured reference for logical deductions in Norinori (のりのり) puzzles

Rules of Norinori

  • The grid is divided into regions (shown by thick borders).
  • Shade exactly 2 cells in every region.
  • Every shaded cell must belong to a domino — a pair of orthogonally adjacent shaded cells.
  • Dominoes may cross region boundaries.

See axioms below for basic operations.

LEMMA 1

Forlorn quarter

In an L-shaped 3-cell region, the cell that would complete the 2×2 square cannot be shaded. The region's domino must pass through the corner cell (since the two ends aren't adjacent), and in either orientation, that diagonal cell is a neighbor of the domino — forced unshaded by Lemma 2.

LEMMA 2

Corner lock

In an L-tromino, if the corner cell is shaded, it must pair with one of the two end cells — it cannot form a cross-region domino.

⟹ Any external neighbor of the corner that is shaded must get its domino from elsewhere.
LEMMA 3

Elbow

In an L-tromino, if the cell past one end is unshaded or out of bounds, then the cell past the corner on the same axis is also unshaded. Both valid domino placements force it unshaded via domino closure.

LEMMA 4

Stash

In an L-tromino, if one end has all its external neighbors unshaded, it can only pair with the corner. If the corner–other end domino is also ruled out, then the surrounded end and the corner are shaded, and the other end is unshaded.

⟹ Tip: this may not be the only valid solution, but it is guaranteed to be the lowest-impact way to satisfy the cell's requirements.
LEMMA 5

Brainstem

When an L-shaped 4-cell region and a straight 3-cell region together fill a 2×3 rectangle (with one extra cell sticking out), the extra cell — the "brainstem" — must be shaded.

⟹ If it weren't, the straight region's domino would sit inside the rectangle, blocking the L-region from forming a valid domino. Contradiction.
LEMMA 6

Clasp

Two parallel, fully adjacent 1×3 regions: the center of each is unshaded, and the outer cells form cross-region dominoes.

⟹ A shaded center would force a neighbor with two shaded neighbors. Contradiction.
LEMMA 7

Friction

If two 3-cell regions share exactly 2 border edges, the one cell in each region that is not adjacent to the other region must be shaded.

⟹ If that non-adjacent cell were unshaded, the other two cells of its region would both need to pair cross-region — but both share adjacency with the same single cell in the opposing region, which can only serve one domino. Contradiction.
LEMMA 7b

Staircase

When an L-tromino sits on top of a straight 3-cell region, the solution is fully determined. The L-tromino's corner excludes the cell below it (Lemma 16), which forces the straight region's domino to the far end. That in turn excludes the L-tromino's inner end, locking in its domino.

⟹ Corollary of Lemma 16 + Axiom 2.
LEMMA 8

Compass

If a shaded cell has two consecutive cardinal neighbors unshaded (e.g. north and east), it must pair with one of the other two. In both cases, the diagonal cell opposite the unshaded pair is forced unshaded by Lemma 2.

⟹ (N,E) unshaded → SW unshaded. Same for the other three rotations.
Axioms (direct consequences of the rules)
AXIOM 1

Two-cell region → both shaded

If a region contains exactly 2 cells, both must be shaded. Since they need to form a domino, the cells must also be orthogonally adjacent — which well-formed puzzles guarantee.

AXIOM 2

Exactly one shaded neighbor

Each shaded cell has exactly one shaded orthogonal neighbor. This follows directly from the domino rule — each shaded cell belongs to exactly one domino.

AXIOM 3

Region saturation

Each region must contain exactly 2 shaded cells.

⟹ Upper bound: 2 cells already shaded → all remaining cells in the region are unshaded.
⟹ Lower bound: Only 2 unshaded cells left in the region → both must be shaded.
AXIOM 4

Isolated region → internal domino

If a region has no cells adjacent to any other region, its domino must be internal.

AXIOM 5

Domino closure — neighbors of a domino are unshaded

Each shaded cell belongs to exactly one domino, meaning it has exactly one shaded neighbor. Once two adjacent shaded cells form a confirmed domino, every other orthogonal neighbor of either cell must be unshaded.

AXIOM 6

Smothered cell

If all four orthogonal neighbors of a cell are unshaded, the cell itself cannot be shaded — it has no domino partner.

AXIOM 7

Squeeze

A shaded cell must have exactly one shaded neighbor. If all but one of a shaded cell's orthogonal neighbors are known to be unshaded (or out-of-bounds), the remaining neighbor must be shaded.

⟹ Especially powerful near grid corners and walls, or wherever other lemmas have eliminated neighbors.
AXIOM 8

Three's a crowd

Three consecutive cells in a line cannot all be shaded — the middle cell would have two shaded neighbors. So if two non-adjacent cells in a line are both shaded, the cell between them is unshaded.

AXIOM 9

Checkerboard parity

Color the grid in a checkerboard pattern (this is a virtual/conceptual overlay, not regions). Every domino — horizontal or vertical — covers exactly one dark and one light cell. Therefore across the whole puzzle: total shaded dark cells = total shaded light cells.

⟹ Use this to rule out impossible configurations when counting shaded cells per color.
